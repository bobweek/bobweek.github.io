The Wavelet transform

  • wavelet - a little wave, \(\psi\), (that starts and stops) unlike the sine/cosine that go on forever.

  • key idea: express functions as sums of this wave and its translates and dilates.

Dilation: \(f_\lambda(x)\equiv\lambda f(\lambda x)\).

  • ex: dilate \(\sin(x)|_{[0,2\pi]}\) by \(2\) and by \(1/2\)

    • \(2\sin(2x)|_{[0,\pi]}\)

    • \(\frac{1}{2}\sin(\frac{x}{2})|_{[0,4\pi]}\)

Dilation gives rise to a “zooming action”.

Def: A function \(\psi\in L^2(\mathbb{R})\) is called a wavelet if the family

\[\psi_{j,k}(x)=2^{j/2}\psi(2^jx-k), \ j,k\in\mathbb{Z}\]

forms an ONB of \(L^2(\mathbb{R})\). If so, the basis is called a wavelet basis.

Proposition: If \(\psi\in L^2(\mathbb{R})\) is a wavelet, then the following reconstruction formula holds:

\[f(x)=\sum_{j,k}\left<f,\psi_{j,k}\right>\psi_{j,k}, \ f\in L^2(\mathbb{R})\]

in \(L^2\). \(\diamond\)

  • The inner products above are the wavelet coefficients of \(f\).

The wavelet transform is given by

\[W f(j,k)\equiv\left<f,\psi_{j,k}\right>=\int_\mathbb{R}f(x)\bar \psi(x)_{j,k}(x)dx.\]

Example: The Haar wavelet \(h(x)\) on the unit interval is given by

\[h(x)\equiv-\chi_{[0,1/2)}+\chi_{[1/2,1)}.\]

The family \(\{h_{j,k}(x)=2^{j/2}h(2^jx-k)\}\) is an ONB for \(L^2(\mathbb{R})\).

Shannon wavelet: The wavelet \(\psi\) is given by

\[\hat\psi(\gamma)\equiv e^{2\pi i \gamma}\chi_{[-1,-1.2)\cup[1/2,1)}(\gamma)\]

which is more of a manifestation for a common technique of constructing wavelets: by constructing the Fourier transform first, and then inverting to get the wavelet. The family \(\{\psi_{j,k}\}\) is called the Shannon basis.

Q: Do we get another ONB via \(\mathscr{F}\)-transforms?

  • Yes! This is just due to the preservation of orthoganality.
  • However, this is no longer a wavelet basis.

Recall \(f(\lambda x)\rightarrow\frac{1}{\lambda}\hat f(\gamma/\lambda)\) and \(f(x+h)\rightarrow\hat f(\gamma)e^{2\pi i h\gamma}\).

Instead, the ONB \(\{\hat\psi_{j,k}\}\) consists of dilations and modulations of \(\hat\psi\) rather than dilations and translations.

  • NOTE: The Haar wavelet is compactly supported, but the Shannon wavelet is not.
