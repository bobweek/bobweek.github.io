Harmonic analysis - 1/25/19

Approximate idetities

A family of functions \(\{K_n\}\) is an approximate identity if

  1. \(\forall \ n\geq1 \ \implies \ \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^\pi K_n(x)dx=1\)

  2. \(\exists \ M \ \forall \ n\geq1 \int|K_n|\leq M\)

  3. For each \(\delta>0\), \(\int_{\delta\leq|x|\leq\pi}|K_n|\rightarrow0\) as \(n\rightarrow\infty\)

Recall: If \(f\in C^k[-\pi,\pi)\) and \(g\) is integrable, then \(f*g\in C^k[-\pi,\pi)\)

Theorem: Let \(\{K_n\}\) be an approximate identity. If \(f\) is integrable on \([-\pi,\pi)\) and continuous at some \(x\), then

\[\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}(K_n*f)(x)=f(x).\]

Furthermore, if \(f\) is cont on \([-\pi,\pi)\) then the convergence is uniform.

Proof: Let \(f\) be cont at \(x\in[-\pi,\pi)\). Then, given \(\epsilon>0,\exists \ \delta>0\) s.t.

\[|f(x-y)-f(x)|<\epsilon\]

when \(|y|<\delta\). Then

\[(K_n*f)(x)-f(x)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^\pi K_n(y)f(x-y)dy-f(x)\left(\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^\pi K_n(y)dy\right)\]

\[=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^\pi[f(x-y)-f(x)]K_n(y)dy\]

\[\implies\frac{1}{2\pi}\left|\int_{-\pi}^\pi[f(x-y)-f(x)]K_n(y)dy\right|\]

\[\leq\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{|y|<\delta}|K_n(y)||f(x-y)-f(x)|dy\]

\[+\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{\delta\leq|y|\leq\pi}|K_n(y)||f(x-y)-f(x)|dy=\star\]

Since \(|f|\) is cont on a cmpct interval, it is bounded. Let \(B\) be a bound for \(|f|\). Then

\[\star<\frac{\epsilon}{2\pi}\int_{|y|\leq\delta}|K_n(y)|dy+\frac{2B}{2\pi}\int_{\delta\leq|y|\leq\pi}|K_n(y)|dy\]

\[\leq\frac{\epsilon M+2B\tilde\epsilon}{2\pi}\]

for any \(\tilde\epsilon>0\). Since \(\epsilon\) and \(\tilde\epsilon\) can be made arbitrarily small given large enough \(n\), we have the result. \(\square\)

Recall

\[S_N(f)=f*D_n, \ \ D_N=\sum_{n=-N}^Ne^{inx}.\]

However, even though \(\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^\pi D_N(x)dx=1\), property 2) of approximate identities does not hold. Thus, \(\{D_N\}\) is not an approximate identity and \(\{S_N\}\) need not converge point-wise.

Look at the Cesaro means

\[\sigma_N(f)=\frac{\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}S_n(f)}{N}\]

\[\frac{\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}f*D_n}{N}=f*\left(\frac{\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}D_n}{N}\right).\]

Def: The \(N\)th Fejer kernal is

\[F_N\equiv\frac{\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}D_n}{N}\]

and hence \(\sigma_N(f)=f*F_N\).

Result: \(\{F_N\}\) is an approximate identity.

Proof:

  1. show that \(F_N(x)=\frac{1}{N}\frac{\sin^2(Nx/2)}{\sin^2(x/2)}\).

  2. To show that \(\{F_N\}\) is an approximate identity we just need to show property 1) and 3) of approximate identities.

Since \(\int D_N(x)=1\) (requires proof) we have \(\int F_N(x)=1\).

  1. Given \(\delta, \exists \ c_\delta\) s.t. \(\sin^2(x/2)\geq c_\delta>0\) if \(\delta\leq|x|\leq\pi\). We get

\[F_N(x)\leq\frac{1}{N c_\delta}\]

which implies \(\int_{\delta\leq|x|\leq\pi}|F_N(x)|dx\rightarrow0\) as \(N\rightarrow\infty\).

Cor: \(\sigma_n(f)\) converges uniformly to \(f\).
