Thought 1

The utilization curve \(u_i(x)\) here is considered fixed across a species, but this need not be restricted to an emergent property of a population or species. Individuals can have utilization curves and these curves can change as the distribution of resources change. That is, they can be plastic. Of course it would be better to assume canalized for each individual. Then their evolution can be modelled quite easily.

We can assume that an individual with phenotype \(z\) will have the resource curve \(u(x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\eta^2}}\exp{\left( -\frac{(x-z)^2}{2\eta^2} \right)}\) where \(\eta^2\) is potentially heritable alongside \(z\). This makes for a two dimensional phenotype. Note that \(z\) and \(x\) will need to be defined on the same space. The biological justification for this is that an animal is likely to use resources that match its phenotype such as beak and seed sizes. We can assume the amount of resources at time \(t\) and location \(x\) in niche-space is \(R_t(x)\) and that

\[\frac{dR_t}{dt}=\lambda_t(K_t-R_t)-C_tR_t\text{ or }\Delta R_t=\lambda_t(K_t-R_t)-C_tR_t\]

so that \(R_t\) converges to \(K_t>0\) at a rate mediated by \(\lambda_t\) when \(C_t=0\). But \(C_t\) will represent total per-capita consumption of the resource, so as long as there are species utilizing location \(x\) at time \(t\), \(C_t(x)>0\).

The fitness of an individual in the absence of all other individuals may be written as \(W=\int u(x)R(x)dx\). Drawing on the finite space example used above to obtain some intuition, we see that if the resources available do not co-occur with the resource utilization curve, then the fitness of the individual will be zero. However, if \(u(x)=cR(x)\) for some positive constant \(c\) then fitness is maximized.

Beginnings

Before we move on to multiple species, let’s consider a population of \(n\) individuals. Each of their utilization curves are denoted by \(u_1(x),u_2(x),\dots,u_n(x)\). Then \(C_t(x)=\sum_iu_i(x)\) so that

\[\Delta R_t=\lambda_t(K_t-R_t)-R_t\sum_i u_i\overset{?}{\rightarrow}\frac{dR_t}{dt}=\lambda_t(K_t-R_t)-R_tn_t\overline{u_t}\]

\[R_\infty=\frac{\lambda K}{1+n\bar u}\dots\text{ non-normal}\dots\text{ a necessary evil?}\]

If there was no capacity to \(R\) so the resource can accumulate towards infinity (which I think is really dumb), then

\[R_\infty=\frac{\lambda}{n\bar u}\dots\text{ suggests diversifying selection}.\]

This is even dumber than I previously imagined!! Of course we get diversifying selection. If all resources are accumulating at a constant rate, then the population will disperse out so no individuals compete with one another. What a dumb model. We might be able to rectify the situation by assuming that \(\lambda\) is Gaussian. In that case it could go either way, the population could explode and diversify off into infinity, or converge to a delta.

Let \(n_t(z)\) be the population density of trait \(z\) at time \(t\). Then

\[\frac{dn_t}{dt}=\ln{\left( \int u(x)R(x)dx \right)}n_t.\]

We can assume \(z\sim\mathcal{N}(\bar z,\sigma^2)\) and \(\eta\sim\ln\mathcal{N}(\overline{\ln\eta},V_\eta)\). We can probably get

\[\frac{d\bar z}{dt}=\frac{\sigma^2}{\psi_z}(?-\bar z), \ \frac{d\overline{\ln\eta}}{dt}=\frac{V_\eta}{\psi_\eta}(?-\overline{\ln\eta}).\]

Or simplify things for now and assume \(\eta\) is constant. If we assume \(R_t=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\omega_t^2}}\exp{\left( -\frac{(\theta_t-z)^2}{2\omega_t^2} \right)}\) then

\[W_i=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi(\eta^2+\omega_t^2)}}\exp{\left( -\frac{(z-\theta_t)^2}{2(\eta^2+\omega_t^2)} \right)}\]

and

\[\overline{W_t}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi(\eta^2+\omega_t^2+\sigma^2)}}\exp{\left( -\frac{(\bar z-\theta_t)^2}{2(\eta^2+\omega_t^2+\sigma^2)} \right)}.\]

Hence

\[\frac{d\bar z_t}{dt}=\frac{\sigma^2}{\eta^2+\omega_t^2+\sigma^2}(\theta_t-\bar z_t).\]

Let’s now set \(K_t=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\omega_t^2}}\exp{\left( -\frac{(\theta_t-z)^2}{2\omega_t^2} \right)}\) instead of \(R_t\) and for starters assume \(\omega_t=\omega\) and \(\theta_t=\theta\) so that \(K_t\) is constant wrt \(t\).
