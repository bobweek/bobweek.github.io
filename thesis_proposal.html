The Coevolutionary Theory of Community Ecology

What is a community?

I accept the very broad and flexible description of a biological community provided in the classic book “Community Ecology” edited by Diamond and Case:

  • “First, the site size may vary from an entire biogeographical region to a one-pint laboratory culture bottle. Second, the number of species may vary from a whole local biota to a tiny fraction of it. Finally, the fraction of the biota may be delineated in various ways: a taxonomic set of species, a life form set, a group of mutualists and their resources, a single trophic level, two interacting trophic levels, or a whole food web.”

I will use the phrase “entire community” to refer to the entire set of biota occurring within a given region. Subsets of an entire community will be referred to by prefixing the word community with the object(s) of interest. For example the term competitive community would refer to a set of species competing for a common set of resources. Likewise a predator-prey community would refer to an extension of a competitive community where the set of resources are replaced with a set of prey species.

Why community ecology?

In my mind, the entire community is the ultimate biological unit of study. We can model the evolution and ecology of a single species, incorporating a mind-numbing amount of detail about mating structure, genetic architecture, spatial dynamics and so on. But even with such a thorough investigation we typically cannot claim to have understood why this particular species is the way that it is because species do not exist in a vacuum. They co-occur with various other species and this geographic overlap inevitably leads to interspecific interactions. To be certain that, for example, the abiotic environment is responsible for the diversity of a species or clade, this hypothesis should be tested against competing ones that claim such diversity is the result of interspecific interactions. Furthermore, to repeat a statement that has been made many times over, any reasonably observant individual who has spent a modest amount of time in natural environments will likely notice patterns such as the co-occurance of a particular plant-fungus species pair or abrudt changes in species composition from one location to another. To understand why such patterns exist it is important to recognize that a pair of interacting species constitutes just one element of an enormous web of interactions across an entire community. How does the nature of each of these pairwise interactions structure the entire community and give rise to patterns commonly witnessed in nature?

Questions

  • How does the type of community (e.g. predator/prey or mutualistic network) correspond to the patterns produced (e.g. trait and abundance distributions)?
    • How many species can be maintained in different types of communities?
      • How does (co)evolution affect this number?
    • What kind of trait distributions can we expect in different types of communities?
      • How does (co)evolution affect this distribution?
    • What kind of abundance distributions can we expect in different types of communities?
      • How does (co)evolution affect this distribution?

  • How common is pairwise coevolution?

  • How sensitive is a community to immigrant species?
    • Will the residents evolve in response or will the new species become “assimilated”?
    • How will the abundances of resident species change?
  • How does space interact with coevolution to produce community structure?
    • What determines the distribution of various species?
    • Does spatial continuity fundamentally differ from discretized space with respect to the dynamics of coevolution and abundance?

  • Under what conditions do specialist species evolve?

  • How does succession proceed while coevolution occurs?
    • If we start with a biologically vacant region that has some abiotic resources, give a regional pool of species, how will the community assemble?
    • Can we expect coevolution to rampant or rare during a succession?

Tangential questions

  • How does plasticity change the answers to each of the above questions?

  • How does phylogenetic signal change the answers to each of the above questions?

  • How does the genetic architecture of the traits involved change the answers to each of the above questions?

Structure of the approach

The idea here does not deviate from traditional reductionist approaches. To analyze a community I plan to first tear it apart into appropriate subunits of study. The atoms of such a reduction are the individuals. By making assumptions about the individuals life-cycle we can derive dynamics of their population. This leads an understanding their metapopulation, their entire species, sets of species, and eventually the entire community.

Pairwise coevolution (2 species)

The theory developed for pairwise coevolution provides the backbone for all (or most) of these investigations. This theory has been constructed exactly as described above. We make certain assumptions at the individual level and have been able to derive the resulting dynamics for entire metapopulations.

Coevolutionary community modules (a few species)

These models extend the pairwise models to a set of 3 or 4 species and will be used to focus on particular aspects of the big picture. There may be some questions that are more appropiately answered by focusing on just a few species, but the point of these models is to provide not much more than a stepping stone to the amalgamation of many species.

Diffuse coevolution (many species)

This will be the primary focus. I hope to develop models that capture random variables representing the number of species, the number of significant pairwise interactions for each species, the strengths of pairwise biotic selection for every interaction, the abundances of each species, etc. I hope that this approach will lead me to a natural way of quantifying the evolutionary stability of a community. This would in turn allow for rigorous investigations of, for example, effect of a species with a particular set of attributes immigrating into the community or how the community will respond to environmental changes. Even with this larger scale approach, I plan only to study subsets of entire communities, such as plant-pollinator or predator-prey communities.
