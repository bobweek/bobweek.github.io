The Coevolutionary Theory of Community Ecology

What is a community?

I accept the very broad and flexible description of a biological community provided in the classic book “Community Ecology” edited by Diamond and Case:

  • “First, the site size may vary from an entire biogeographical region to a one-pint laboratory culture bottle. Second, the number of species may vary from a whole local biota to a tiny fraction of it. Finally, the fraction of the biota may be delineated in various ways: a taxonomic set of species, a life form set, a group of mutualists and their resources, a single trophic level, two interacting trophic levels, or a whole food web.”

I will use the phrase “entire community” to refer to the entire set of biota occurring within a given region. Subsets of an entire community will be referred to by prefixing the word community with the object(s) of interest. For example the term competitive community would refer to a set of species competing for a common set of resources. Likewise a predator-prey community would refer to an extension of a competitive community where the set of resources are replaced with a set of prey species.

Why community ecology?

In my mind, the entire community is the ultimate unit of study in evolution and ecology. We can model the evolution and ecology of a single species, incorporating a mind-numbing amount of detail about mating structure, genetic architecture, spatial dynamics and so on. But even with such a thorough investigation we typically cannot claim to have understood why this particular species is the way that it is because species do not exist in a vacuum. They co-occur with various other species and this geographic overlap inevitably leads to interspecific interactions. Of course there are models that have been developed to capture the evolutionary and ecological dynamics of interacting species. However, these models are typically limited to a small number of species even though we know entire communities do not generally consist of just a few species. Incorporating such facts into our methods can alter the conclusions drawn. To be certain that, for example, the abiotic environment is responsible for the diversity of a species or clade, this hypothesis should be tested against competing ones such as the hypothesis that the observed diversity is the result of interspecific interactions. Furthermore, to repeat a statement that has been made many times over, any reasonably observant individual who has spent a modest amount of time in natural environments will likely notice patterns such as the co-occurance of particular species of plants and fungi or the abrudt changes in species composition from one location to another. To understand why such patterns exist it is important to recognize that a pair of interacting species constitutes just one element in an enormous web of interactions across an entire community. How does the nature of each of these pairwise interactions structure the entire community and give rise to patterns commonly witnessed in nature?

Questions

Here I provide a list of questions relating coevolution to community ecology.

  • How common is pairwise coevolution?
    • Given an index of coevolutionary selection, what is the distribution of this index across a community?
    • How does this distribution change with respect to community type?
  • How does succession proceed in the presence of coevolution?
    • If we start with a biologically vacant region that has some abiotic resources, give a regional pool of species, how will the community assemble?
    • Can we expect coevolution to rampant or rare during a succession?
    • How sensitive is a community to immigrant species?
      • Will the residents evolve in response or will the new species become “assimilated”?
  • Under what conditions do specialist species evolve?
    • Specifically, in plant/pollinator communities, theory suggests that coevolution will result in a higher degree of generalism which in turn makes for a more robust community. Then why do specialists evolve?
    • In many communities there are lots of boring species (species with common traits) and few species with exaggerated traits. Can this pattern be explained by the distribution of coevolution in a community?

Note that the answer to the first question is relevant to answering both subsequent questions. Futhermore, there is significant interplay between the latter two questions. For example, the evolution of specialists during the colonization of a novel landscape can alter the pattern of succession.

Structure of the approach

The idea here does not deviate from traditional reductionist approaches. To analyze a community I plan to first tear it apart into appropriate subunits of study. The atoms of such a reduction are the individuals. By making assumptions about the life-cycle of individuals we can derive ecological and evolutionary dynamics of their population. This leads to an understanding of their metapopulation, their entire species, sets of species, sets of guilds and eventually the entire community.

Pairwise coevolution (2 species)

The theory developed for pairwise coevolution provides the backbone for all (or most) of these investigations. This theory has been constructed exactly as described above. We make certain assumptions at the individual level and have been able to derive the resulting dynamics for entire metapopulations. The case of fixed population sizes has been thoroughly investigated. However, since evolutionary and demographic dynamics are coupled through fitness and the form of fitness has already been specified, extending this work to fluctuating population sizes will be straightforward. In particular, for a pair of interacting species with mean traits \(\bar z_1\), \(\bar z_2\) and population sizes \(n_1,n_2\), I expect to obtain dynamics of the form

\[\Delta\bar z_i=f_{z,i}(\bar z_1,\bar z_2,n_1,n_2), \ \Delta n_i=f_{n,i}(\bar z_1,\bar z_2,n_1,n_2).\]

Coevolutionary community modules (a few species)

These models extend the pairwise models to a set of 3 or 4 species and will be used to focus on particular aspects of the big picture. There may be some questions that are more appropiately answered by focusing on just a few species, but the point of these models is to provide not much more than a stepping stone to the amalgamation of many species. For \(k\) species, I anticipate these models to be a literal extension of the pairwise models, taking the form

\[\Delta\bar z_i=f_{z,i}(\bar z_1,\dots,\bar z_k,n_1,\dots,n_k), \ \Delta n_i=f_{n,i}(\bar z_1,\dots,\bar z_k,n_1,\dots,n_k).\]

Diffuse coevolution (many species)

This will be the primary focus. Instead of explicitly modelling the dynamics of each species within the community I will take the so called mean-field approach by assuming population sizes and mean traits are iid within a guild. Parameters capturing interaction strengths can be held as fixed constants or also modelled as random variables. I hope that this approach will lead me to a natural way of quantifying the evolutionary stability of a community. This would in turn allow for rigorous investigations of, for example, effect of a species with a particular set of attributes immigrating into the community or how the community will respond to environmental changes. Even with this larger scale approach, I plan only to study subsets of entire communities, such as plant-pollinator or predator-prey-resource communities.

In this framework the models will be composed of a set of stochastic processes \(\{(\overline {Z}_t^{(i)},N_t^{(i)}):i=1,\dots,k\}\) with each pair representing the mean traits and population sizes of the species in guild \(i\). To provide a more detailed picture, if there are \(k\) guilds, each guild \(i\) having a set of species denoted by \(S_i\), then, in terms of probability theory, our sample space will be \(\Omega=S_1\times\cdots\times S_k\) so that \(\omega=(s_1,\dots,s_k)\in\Omega\) specifies a particular combination of species among the guilds. So \(\{(\overline {Z}_t^{(i)},N_t^{(i)})\}(\omega)=\{(\overline {Z}_t^{(1)}(s_1),N_t^{(1)}(s_1)),\dots,(\overline {Z}_t^{(k)}(s_k),N_t^{(k)}(s_k))\}\) is a sample path representing the evolution of mean trait and population size for a specific set of species.
