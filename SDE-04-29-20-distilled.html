Stochastic Integration

Bob Week

04-29-2020

The Goal

  • How do we make sense of \(\left\{\begin{matrix} dX=b(X,t)dt+B(X,t)dW \\ X(0)=X_0 \end{matrix}\right.\) ?

  • Rewrite as \(X(t)=X_0+\int_0^tb(X,s)ds+\int_0^tB(X,s)dW(s)\).

  • Can make sense of \(\int_0^tb(X,s)ds\) for each fixed \(\omega\).

    • That is, for each sample path.

  • But what is \(\int_0^tB(X,s)dW(s)\) ?

    • Evans suggests we try a Riemann sum.

Riemann sum approximation of

\(\int_0^T W(t)dW(t)\)

  1. \(P:=\{0=t_0<t_1<\cdots<t_m=T\}\) is a partition of \([0,T]\) with mesh size \(|P|:=\max_k|t_{k+1}-t_k|\).

  2. For \(\lambda\in[0,1]\) set \(\tau_k:=(1-\lambda)t_k+\lambda t_{k+1}\) so that \(\tau_k\in[t_k,t_{k+1}]\).

  3. The Riemann sum approximation is \[R(P,\lambda)=\sum_{k=0}^{m-1}W(\tau_k)\big(W(t_{k+1})-W(t_k)\big)\]

What happens as \(|P|\to0\) for fixed \(\lambda\)?

  • Analogous to Riemann integrals, we might expect \(\int_0^TW(t)dW(t)\)

  • But this is an integral with respect to a function instead of \(dt\).

  • Let’s try to make sense of as a Riemann-Stieltjes integral

Riemann-Stieltjes integral

  • A generalization of Riemann integration.

  • Set \(P_n:=\{a=t_0^n<\cdots<t_{m_n}^n=b\}\) such that \(\lim_{n\to\infty}|P_n|=0\).

  • Pick \(\tau_i^n\) so that \(t_i^n\leq\tau_i^n\leq t_{i+1}^n\).

  • Define Riemann-Stieltjes integral of \(g\) against \(f\) as

  • \[\int_0^Tg(t)df(t):=\lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=0}^{m_n-1}g(\tau_i^n)(f(t_{i+1})-f(t_i))\]

  • Requires \(f\) to have finite total variation.

Total variation

  • Consider the set \(\mathcal{P}\) of all partitions of \([0,T]\).

  • Total variation for a function \(f:[0,T]\to\mathbb{R}\) is

  • \[V(f):=\sup_{P\in\mathcal{P}}\sum_{i=0}^{m_P-1}|f(t_{i+1})-f(t_i)|\] where \(m_p\) is the number of intervals in \(P\).

  • Since \(V(W)=+\infty\), we cannot define \(\int_0^TW(t)dW(t)\) as a Riemann-Stieltjes integral.

  • However, \(R(P,\lambda)\) does converge as \(|P|\to0\)

Quadratic variation (QV)

  • Instead of considering total variation, we find the quadratic variation of Brownian Motion satisfies

  • \[Q_n:=\sum_{k=1}^{m_n-1}\Big(W(t_{k+1}^n)-W(t_k^n)\Big)^2\to T\]

  • More precisely, \(\lim_{n\to\infty}\mathbb{E}\left[\big(Q_n-T\big)^2\right]=0\).

Scaling relationship curiousity

  • Evans states the result on QV partially explains \((dW)^2\approx dt\).

  • To illustrate, recall the Riemann integral is given by \[\sum_{k=0}^{m_n-1}t^n_{k+1}-t^n_k\to\int_0^Tdt=T.\]

  • Then the QV result suggests \(\int_0^T(dW)^2=\int_0^Tdt\).

Returning to the question:

What happens to \(R(P,\lambda)\) when \(|P|\to0\)?

  • We can use the QV result and properties of Brownian Motion to show \[\lim_{n\to\infty}R_n=\frac{W(T)^2}{2}+\left(\lambda-\frac{1}{2}\right)T\]

  • Setting \(\lambda=0\) coincides with the Itô integral \[\int_0^T WdW=\frac{1}{2}\Big(W(T)^2-T\Big).\]

  • Setting \(\lambda=\frac{1}{2}\) coincides with the Stratonovich integral \[\int_0^TW\circ dW=\frac{W(T)^2}{2}.\]

Why is \(\lambda=0\) a sensible choice?

  • Consider the SDE \[dX=a(X,t)dt+B(X,t)dW\] and ODE \[\frac{dx}{dt}=a(x,t).\]

  • Given solutions to both exist, we might expect \(\mathbb{E}(X(t))=x(t)\).

  • This only holds for \(\lambda=0\).

Square-integrable processes

  • If \(G\) satisfies \(\mathbb{E}\left(\int_0^TG^2(t)dt\right)<+\infty\), then we write \[G\in\mathbb{L}^2(0,T).\]

    • We’ll see Itô’s integral maps \(\mathbb{L}^2(0,T)\) processes to random variables.

Step processes

Approximations of \(\mathbb{L}^2(0,T)\) processes

  • Before we define Itô’s integral for \(\mathbb{L}^2(0,T)\) processes, we look at simpler processes.

  • If there is a partition \({\{t_k}\}_{k=1}^{m}\) of \([0,T]\) and a set of random variables \(\{G_0,\dots,G_{m-1}\}\) such that

    • \(G\in\mathbb{L}^2(0,T)\) satisfies \[G(t)=G_k, \ t\in[t_k,t_{k+1}), \ k=0,\dots,m-1,\]

    • then \(G\) is called a step process.

  • We can approximate \(\mathbb{L}^2(0,T)\) processes arbitrarily close with step processes.

Approximating \(\mathbb{L}^2(0,T)\) processes

Itô’s integral

For step processes

  • For step processes \(G\) we define the Itô integral by \[\mathscr{I}(G):=\int_0^TGdW:=\sum_{k=0}^{m-1}G_k\big(W(t_{k+1})-W(t_k)\big).\]

Properties of Itô’s integral

For step processes

  • For step processes \(G,H\) we can prove

    1. \(\int_0^TaG+bHdW=a\int_0^TGdW+b\int_0^THdW\dots\) linearity,

    2. \(\mathbb{E}\left(\int_0^TGdW\right)=0\dots\) martingale property,

    3. \(\mathbb{E}\left[\left(\int_0^TGdW\right)^2\right]=\mathbb{E}\left(\int_0^TG^2dW\right)\dots\) isometry.

Itô’s integral

For \(\mathbb{L}^2(0,T)\) processes

  • For any \(G\) in \(\mathbb{L}^2(0,T)\) we can find a sequence \(\{G_n\}\) of step processes that converges to \(G\).

  • The isometry property of Itô’s integral for step processes implies

  • \[\mathbb{E}\left[\left(\int_0^TG_n-G_mdW\right)^2\right]=\mathbb{E}\left(\int_0^T(G_n-G_m)^2dt\right)\to0.\]

  • Hence, we define Itô’s integral for \(G\) as

  • \[\mathscr{I}(G):=\lim_{n\to\infty}\int_0^TG_ndW\]

Properties of Itô’s integral

  • In analogy with step processes, we find for all \(G,H\in\mathbb{L}^2(0,T)\)

    1. \(\mathscr{I}(aG+bH)=a\mathscr{I}(G)+b\mathscr{I}(H)\),

    2. \(\mathbb{E}\left[\mathscr{I}(G)\right]=0\),

    3. \(\mathbb{E}\left[\mathscr{I}(G)^2\right]=\mathbb{E}\left(\int_0^TG^2dt\right).\)

  • However, we now have the additional property

    1. \(\mathbb{E}\left[\mathscr{I}(G)\mathscr{I}(H)\right]=\mathbb{E}\left(\int_0^TGHdt\right)\dots\) orthogonality.
