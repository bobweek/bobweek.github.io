Evolution

In the first half of this document we assume asexual reproduction and perfect heritability in order to simplify our derivations. It turns out that many of the results proven extend to identical, or very similar, results for the cases of sexual reproduction and imperfect heritability.

Populations of Types

In terms of modelling, a population can be very abstract. Its concept is not restricted to a set of living things, as in the biological concept of a population. Here a population can be a set of biological populations, a set of species, replicating molecules, franchises, languages and so on. The members of the population may not be uniform, but can consist of various different types. For example, we can consider populations of individuals categorized by their phenotype or populations of species categorized by clades. We now derive how the distribution of types within a population fluctuates due to differential fitness, ie, selection.

A finite number of types

Dynamics in continuous time

Consider a population of \(T\) different types, each type \(\gamma\) with \(\mathfrak{n}_t(\gamma)\) individuals at time \(t\). If we denote \(\phi_t(\gamma)\) to be the proportion of individuals within the whole population of type \(\gamma\) at time \(t\) among the population, we have \(\phi_t(\gamma)=\frac{\mathfrak{n}_t(\gamma)}{n_t}\) where \(n_t=\sum_{\gamma=1}^T\mathfrak{n}_t(\gamma)\). Denote the expected fitness of each individual of type \(\gamma\) as \(W_t(\gamma)\) and assume individuals of type \(\gamma\) only produce other individuals of type \(\gamma\). That is, the type of an individual is a perfectly heritable trait. Then, assuming reproduction events are distributed uniformly across generations, focusing on expected dynamics and adopting the notation \(D_t\) in place of \(\frac{d}{dt}\), we have (from our notes on population dynamics)

\[D_tn_t=D_t\sum_\gamma\mathfrak{n}_t(\gamma)=\sum_\gamma\mathfrak{n}_t(\gamma)\ln W_t(\gamma)=\overline{\ln W_t}n_t.\]

\[D_t\phi_t(\gamma)=D_t\frac{\mathfrak{n}_t(\gamma)}{n_t}=\frac{n_tD_t\mathfrak{n}_t(\gamma)-\mathfrak{n}_t(\gamma)D_tn_t}{n_t^2}\]

\[=\frac{\mathfrak{n}_t(\gamma)\ln W_t(\gamma)-\mathfrak{n}_t(\gamma)\overline{\ln W_t}}{n_t}=\phi_t(\gamma)\left( \ln W_t(\gamma)-\overline{\ln W_t} \right).\]

From here I will use the notation \(m_t(\gamma)=\ln W_t(\gamma)\) and \(\bar m_t=\overline{\ln W_t}\). Using these symbols, we have

\[D_t\phi_t=\phi_t(m_t-\bar m_t)\]

which is known as the replicator equation in evolutionary game theory (see Nowak & Page 2002). In general, we can solve for \(\phi_t(\gamma)\) using

\[\phi_t(\gamma)=\phi_0(\gamma)\exp\left(\int_0^t m_s(\gamma)-\bar m_sds\right).\]

Just as a sanity check, we have

\[D_t\mathfrak{n}_t=n_tD_t\phi_t+\phi_tD_tn_t\]

\[=n_t\phi_t\left( m_t-\bar m_t \right)+\phi_tn_t\bar m_t=n_t\phi_tm_t=\mathfrak{n}_tm_t.\]

Dynamics in discrete time

\[n_{t+1}=\sum_\gamma \mathfrak{n}_{t+1}(\gamma)=\sum_\gamma W_t(\gamma)\mathfrak{n}_t(\gamma)=\frac{n_t}{n_t}\sum_\gamma W_t(\gamma)\mathfrak{n}_t(\gamma)\]

\[=n_t\sum W_t(\gamma)\frac{\mathfrak{n}_t(\gamma)}{n_t}=n_t\sum W_t(\gamma)\phi_t(\gamma)=n_t\overline{W}_t\]

\[\phi_{t+1}(\gamma)=\frac{\mathfrak{n}_{t+1}(\gamma)}{n_{t+1}}=\frac{\mathfrak{n}_t(\gamma)W_t(\gamma)}{n_t\overline{W}_t}=\phi_t(\gamma)\frac{W_t(\gamma)}{\overline{W}_t}\]

\[\implies\phi_{t+1}(\gamma)-\phi_t(\gamma)=\left( \frac{W_t(\gamma)}{\overline{W}_t}-1 \right)\phi_t(\gamma)\]

  • Neat Observation 1: Compare the above to the Bayesian update; Given a hypothesis \(H_n(x)\) and a likelihood \(L(x)\), Bayes theorem provides the updated hypothesis \(H_{n+1}\) as

\[H_{n+1}(x)=\frac{H_n(x)L(x)}{\int H_n(x)L(x)dx}=H_n(x)\frac{L(x)}{\overline L}\]

  • Neat Observation 2: The fundamental theorem of calculus states

\[\int_{t_0}^{t_1}D_sn_sds=n_{t_1}-n_{t_0}.\]

Thus

\[\Delta n_t=n_{t+1}-n_t=\int_t^{t+1}D_sn_sds\]

\[=\int_t^{t+1}\bar m_sn_sds.\]

Infinite number of types

We now consider dynamics in the case where the set of types is identified with the real line \(\mathbb{R}\). Since each individual is associated with one value in \(\mathbb{R}\), a finite population will be reflected by a finite subset of \(\mathbb{R}\). Since we are not yet considering processes that generate new types, the situation reduces to a population with a finite number of types. We must therefore treat the case of an uncountably infinite population where each value in \(\mathbb{R}\) has a density (as opposed to abundance) in the population. In this case we denote the types by \(z\in\mathbb{R}\) (as opposed to \(\gamma\)) and their density by \(\phi_t(z)\) at time \(t\). Since the result

\[D_t\phi_t(z)=\phi_t(z)(m_t(z)-\bar m_t)\]

is independent of the population size \(n_t\), this formula holds for \(n_t\rightarrow\infty\). In what follows, the above equation is the key tool for deriving our formulas of evolutionary dynamics.

Response in Mean and Variance

For the sake of simplicity, we begin with results for a continuum of types taking values in \(\mathbb{R}\) (and hence \(n_t=\infty\)). Any quantity \(Q(z)\) that satisfies

\[\int_\mathbb{R}|Q(z)|\phi_t(z)dz<\infty\]

is said to have a finite populational average. We denote this average by

\[\overline{Q}_t\equiv\int_\mathbb{R}Q(z)\phi_t(z)dz.\]

We will always assume both Wrightian and Malthusian fitnesses have finite populational averages. We denote by \(\bar z_t\) the average value of the population at time \(t\) and \(\sigma_t^2\), an exception to our notation, the variance of values at time \(t\). That is, we make the following definitions

\[\bar z_t\equiv\int_\mathbb{R}z\phi_t(z)dz, \ \ \ \sigma_t^2\equiv\int_\mathbb{R}(\bar z_t-z)^2\phi_t(z)dz.\]

Clearly, we will be assuming the mean and variance remain finite in our populations. Using this notation along with the above results and assumptions we can derive dynamics for the mean and variance of values in a population as follows

Continuous Time

\[D_t\bar z_t=D_t\int_\mathbb{R}z\phi_t(z)dz=\int_\mathbb{R}z D_t\phi_t(z)dz=\int_\mathbb{R}z(m_t(z)-\bar m_t)\phi_t(z)dz\]

\[=\overline{zm_t}-\bar z\bar m_t=\boxed{\mathrm{Cov}(z,m_t)}\]

\[D_t\sigma_t^2=D_t\int_\mathbb{R}(\bar z_t-z)^2\phi_t(z)dz=\int_\mathbb{R}2(\bar z_t-z)\phi_t(z)D_t\bar z_t+(\bar z_t-z)^2D_t\phi_t(z)dz\]

\[=\int_\mathbb{R}(\bar z_t-z)^2(m_t(z)-\bar m_t)\phi_t(z)dz=\overline{(\bar z_t-z)^2m_t}-\overline{(\bar z_t-z)^2}\bar m_t\]

\[=\boxed{\mathrm{Cov}((\bar z_t-z)^2,m_t)}.\]

Gaussian population

We have

\[\phi_t(z)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma_t^2}}\exp\left(-\frac{(\bar z_t-z)^2}{2\sigma_t^2}\right).\]

With this information we can use Fubini’s theorem (since \(\int|m_t\phi_t|<\infty\)) to state

\[\frac{\partial\bar m_t}{\partial\bar z_t} =\int_\mathbb{R}\frac{\partial}{\partial\bar z_t}m_t(z)\phi_t(z)dz\]

\[=\int_\mathbb{R}m_t\frac{\partial\phi_t}{\partial\bar z_t}+\frac{\partial m_t}{\partial\bar z_t}\phi_tdz\]

\[=\int_\mathbb{R}m_t\frac{z-\bar z_t}{\sigma_t^2}\phi_tdz+\overline{\frac{\partial m_t}{\partial\bar z_t}}\]

\[=\frac{\overline{m_tz_t}-\bar m_t\bar z_t}{\sigma_t^2}+\overline{\frac{\partial m_t}{\partial\bar z_t}}\]

\[=\frac{1}{\sigma_t^2}\mathrm{Cov}(z,m_t)+\overline{\frac{\partial m_t}{\partial\bar z_t}}\]

\[=\frac{1}{\sigma_t^2}\frac{d\bar z_t}{dt}+\overline{\frac{\partial m_t}{\partial\bar z_t}}.\]

Hence, for Gaussian populations,

\[\boxed{\frac{d\bar z_t}{dt}=\sigma_t^2\left(\frac{\partial\bar m_t}{\partial\bar z_t}-\overline{\frac{\partial m_t}{\partial\bar z_t}}\right)}.\]

We can perform a similar maneuver to find

\[\frac{\partial\bar m_t}{\partial\sigma_t^2} =\int_\mathbb{R}m_t\frac{\partial\phi_t}{\partial\sigma_t^2}+\frac{\partial\bar m_t}{\partial\sigma_t^2}\phi_tdz\]

\[=\int_\mathbb{R}m_t\frac{(z-\bar z_t)^2-\sigma_t^2}{2\sigma_t^4}\phi_tdz +\overline{\frac{\partial m_t}{\partial\sigma_t^2}}\]

\[=\frac{\mathrm{Cov}((z-\bar z)^2,m_t)}{2\sigma_t^4}+\overline{\frac{\partial m_t}{\partial\sigma_t^2}}\]

\[=\frac{1}{2\sigma_t^4}\frac{d\sigma_t^2}{dt}+\overline{\frac{\partial m_t}{\partial\sigma_t^2}}\]

which implies

\[\boxed{\frac{d\sigma_t^2}{dt}=2\sigma_t^4\left(\frac{\partial\bar m_t}{\partial\sigma_t^2} -\overline{\frac{\partial m_t}{\partial\sigma_t^2}}\right)}.\]

These results are very convenient, and often times natural populations obey normality, although a suitable transformation may be required (especially a log-transform for non-negative traits such as height). However, to follow mathematical rigor, there is no gurantee that the distribution remains Gaussian after selection has acted. In fact, we can show that this only only holds when \(W_t(z)\) is either Gaussian or exponential with respect to \(z\). That is, \(W\) is either of the form

\[W_t(z)=C_t\exp\left(-\frac{(z-\theta_t)^2}{2\omega_t^2}\right)\]

or

\[W_t(z)=C_t\exp\left(\nu_t z\right).\]

The latter choice has the disadvantage of not being bounded, but makes a decent candidate for unconstrained directional selection. For the first option, one finds

\[\bar m_t=\int_\mathbb{R}\ln C_t-\frac{(z-\theta_t)^2}{2\omega_t^2}\phi_tdz\]

\[=\ln C_t-\frac{(\bar z_t-\theta_t)^2+\sigma_t^2}{2\omega^2}.\]

Hence

\[\frac{d\bar z_t}{dt}=\frac{\sigma_t^2}{\omega_t^2}(\theta_t-\bar z_t)\]

\[\frac{d\sigma_t^2}{dt}=-\frac{\sigma_t^2}{\omega_t^2}\sigma_t^2.\]

Repeating for the model of directional selection, one finds

\[\bar m_t=\int_\mathbb{R}\ln C_t+\nu_t z\phi_tdz=\ln C_t+\nu_t\bar z_t\]

\[\frac{d\bar z_t}{dt}=\sigma_t^2\nu_t\]

\[\frac{d\sigma_t^2}{dt}=0.\]

Thus, we have some first results showing qualitative differences in the dynamics of variation.

These results depend on more than the assumptions of Gaussian populations and fitness taking one of the two forms mentioned above. They also require perfect heritability so that phenotypic variation and additive genetic variation are equivalent. Along these lines, mutation is also ignored, which explains why these results indicate phenotypic variance never increases. We will relax these assumptions later using the notion of a breeding value for continuous characters. Depending on the mating structure, distribution of mutations and so on, we will gain a more complete picture of the dynamics of the distribution of phenotypes within a population. For now, we conclude our results focusing solely on selection with analogs in discrete time.

The inseparability of evolution and ecology

As we saw, the evolutionary dynamics of a population were derived from the demographic dynamics of the various types within that population. The two sets of dynamics are entangled via fitness. The population as a whole grows as a function of mean fitness while evolution occurs due to the structure of fitness across types, which in turn manipulates mean fitness and hence the dynamics of demography. Hence, one cannot, in general, have evolution without ecology. Of course, in the case of infinite populations, this argument in vacuous. However, I know of no such populations.

Reproduction

So far I have assumed that inheritance is perfect so that offspring resemble their parents exactly. The existence of sex in the above theory is ambiguous. One could hold the perspective that individuals reproduce asexually or that individuals only mate with only other individuals of their exact type, creating \(2\mathcal{W}\) offspring, one set for each member of the mating pair. Yet another way to look at this theory is that it tracks just one gender so that, for example, daughters resemble their mothers perfectly and their fathers make no contribution to their type. Here I will explicitly model sexual reproduction by beginning with random mating. I will first focus on discrete time dynamics for a continuum of types indexed by \(\mathbb{R}\). Later I will extend these results to continuous time dynamics and a more general mating pattern that encompasses everything from absolutely assortative mating to random mating to absolutely disassortative mating.

Pure Random Mating

Consider a population of \(N\) hermaphroditic individuals with traits \(\{z_1,\dots,z_N\}\). The trait of an offspring \(z_o\) will be its midparent. Because the population is finite, there is a finite number \(k\) of possible midparents. Denote this set of midparents by \(M=\{m_1,\dots,m_k\}\). Each midparent has a particular probability of occuring which is the probability of choosing two parents whose average is equal to that midparent. Denote \(w_i\) for the probability of midparent \(m_i\). Assuming perfect heritability, the offspring trait distribution will have \(w_i\) as its distribution.

Our goal is calculate a formula for \(w_i\). Let \(P_i\) be the set of all parental phenotypes whose average with some parental phenotype (possibly itself) is \(m_i\). If we think of \(m_i\) as the result of drawing two random parents (with replacement) then the \(m_i\) will be distributed as a multinomial with \(N\) categories and 2 trials. Writing \(\phi_p(z)\) as the frequency of trait \(z\) in the parental distribution we have

\[w_i=2\sum_{\substack{z\in P_i}}\phi_p(z)\phi_p(2m_i-z).\]

To simplify our calculations, assume an infinite population so that the set of possible midparents becomes uncountably infinite and their distribution becomes continuous instead of discrete. We will adjust our notation to reflect this so that \(w(m)\) is the probability density for the offspring trait \(m\) (which in this case is equivalent to the midparent, hence the \(m\)) whose domain is now all of \(\mathbb{R}\). The above summation becomes the integral

\[w(m)=2\int_\mathbb{R}\phi_p(z)\phi_p(2m-z)dz.\]

Assuming the parental population is distributed normally with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\), we have

\[w(m)=2\int_\mathbb{R} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\exp\left( -\frac{(\mu-x)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right) \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\exp\left( -\frac{(\mu-(2m-x))^2}{2\sigma^2} \right) dx\]

\[=2\int_\mathbb{R} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\exp\left( -\frac{(\mu-x)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right) \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\exp\left( -\frac{((2m-\mu)-x)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right) dx\]

\[=2\int_\mathbb{R} \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi\sigma^2}}\exp\left( -\frac{(2\mu-2m)^2}{4\sigma^2} \right) \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\exp\left( -\frac{(m-x)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right) dx\]

\[=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi\sigma^2}}\exp\left( -\frac{(\mu-m)^2}{\sigma^2} \right).\]

Hence, with just random mating \(\Delta\mu=0\) and \(\Delta\sigma^2=-\frac{\sigma^2}{2}\).

Assortative and Negative Mating

The key here is to define assortative mating as a positive correlation between the types of mating pairs. We might jump to disassortative mating as a negative correlation between the types of mating pairs but this is not quite right. If mating pairs are negatively correlated, then when the phenotype of one member of the pair tends to be large, the phenotype of the other member tends to be small. So this is a restricted notion of disassortative mating. I’ll attempt to work out a more general picture of disassortative mating in the next section. For now, instead of referring to the situation of negative correlation disassortative mating, I’ll refer to it as negative mating. I’ll use \(\rho\) to denote the correlation between types of mating pairs.

Let \(X\) and \(Y\) denote the phenotypes of a randomly chosen mating pair. I’ll write \(\bar z\) as the mean phenotype of their distribution and \(\sigma^2\) for the phenotypic variance. If we assume it’s offspring \(Z\) is the midparent then \(Z=\frac{X+Y}{2}\). The mean of the offpsring population will be \(\mathbb{E}(Z)=\frac{1}{2}(\mathbb{E}(X)+\mathbb{E}(Y))=\bar z\). Hence, this model of mating does not perturb the mean phenotype. However

\[\mathrm{Var}(Z)=\frac{1}{4}\left( \mathrm{Var}(X)+\mathrm{Var}(Y)+2\mathrm{Cov}(X,Y) \right)=\frac{1}{2}\left( \sigma^2+\mathrm{Cov}(X,Y) \right)\]

Since \(\mathrm{Cov}(X,Y)=\rho\sigma^2\) we have \(\mathrm{Var}(Z)=\frac{\sigma^2}{2}(1+\rho)\). When \(\rho=0\) mating between types occurs at random and we get the same result as above. When \(\rho=1\), we have perfectly assortative mating and the phenotypic variance remains the same. When \(\rho=-1\) the variance disappears as each mating pair will consist of exactly complementary types and thus all offspring will have the same phenotype.

We can use this result to derive dynamics of the phenotypic distribution \(\phi_t\), but before we dive into the general case, let’s consider the case where both parental and offspring phenotypic distributions are normal. Then, knowing from statistics that the normal distribution is self-conjugate, we can hypothesize the dynamics to take the form

\[\phi_{t+1}=\frac{\psi\phi_t}{\int_\mathbb{R}\psi\phi_tdz}\]

where \(\psi\) is a normal density. If \(\psi\) has mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\nu^2\), then we can use the handy fact that \(\phi_{t+1}\) will be a normal density with the respective mean and variance

\[\frac{\nu^2\bar z+\sigma^2\mu}{\nu^2+\sigma^2}, \ \frac{\nu^2\sigma^2}{\nu^2+\sigma^2}.\]

Since we know the mean doesn’t change under this model of reproduction it must be that \(\mu=\bar z\). We also know the offspring variance and setting it equal to the above result gives

\[\frac{\sigma^2}{2}(1+\rho)=\frac{\nu^2\sigma^2}{\nu^2+\sigma^2}.\]

Solving for \(\nu^2\) provides

\[\frac{2}{1+\rho}=1+\frac{\nu^2}{\sigma^2}\implies\frac{1-\rho}{1+\rho}\sigma^2=\nu^2.\]

The continuous time version of the above Bayesian update is the replicator equation;

\[\frac{d\phi_t}{d t}=\phi_t(\ln\psi_t-\overline{\ln\psi_t}).\]

Particulate Inheritance

A little excerpt from Warren J. Ewens:

“It is the the ‘quantal’ nature of the gene that leads to the stability behavior described by the Hardy-Weinberg law. It is thus interesting that the year in which the Mendelia theory was rediscovered, 1900, was the same year as the introduction of the quantum theory in physics. Both theories have been fundamental and crucial in their respective spheres. One can even claim that if there is intelligent life, that is, life that has evolved via natural selection, elsewhere in the universe, the hereditary mechanism involved must be a quantal, maybe a Mendelian, one, since otherwise it is not clear how the variation necessary for evolution by natural selection can be maintained.”

From this very insightful quote we see one enormous gap in the thoery developed here; it lacks a mechanism of particulate inheritance. In fact, the engine driving the result of reproduction here is blending inheritance. However, it is widely known that the combination of blending inheritance and random mating reduces the variance of a population far too quickly.

Mutation

From my perspective, mutation is a force that causes imperfect replication. That is, the offspring of an individual may not resemble their parent perfectly and this difference is called mutation. This fits the general sense of the word which is alteration, change, or to vary. However, in biology the word mutation refers to a very specific process involving the permament substitution of nucleotide base-pairs. Since I operate at a more abstract level, I will abuse this custom and use the word mutation as described above, referring to imperfect replication. My goal is not to reproduce the minutiae of molecular detail, but to capture the process of imperfect heritability in a tractable way. I believe this will lead me to a leaner, more general theory.

Finally, before we dive in, I just would like to remind myself that the following is not restricted to common phenotypes such as height, toxicity, etc, but also to location. We can think of the different types as either discrete locations or a continuum of geographical space. In this case mutation is nothing more than dispersion.

Returning to a population of \(T\) types, let’s now suppose that there is a probability \(P_t(i,j)\) that an individual of type \(i\) produces an offspring of type \(j\). Then, if \(W(i)\) is the expected fitness of an individual of type \(i\), this individual will on average produce \(W(i)P_t(i,j)\) individuals of type \(j\) and \(W(i)P_t(i,i)\) individuals of type \(i\). The expected dynamics for each type is then

\[n_{t+1}(i)=\sum_{j=0}^TP_t(j,i)W(j)n_t(j)\]

If we write \(\mathbf{n}_t\) as a row vector denoting the sizes of each type and \(\tilde P\) for a matrix whose entries are \(W_{t+1}(j)P_t(i,j)\), we have

\[\mathbf{n}_{t+1}=\mathbf{n}_t \tilde P.\]

From here we can generate all sorts of madness by conjuring up quirky combinations of \(W\) and \(P\). My interest, however, lies in something more docile. I’m primarily interested in continuously varying traits. The above only works for at most a countable number of types. For a continuum suppose an individual with trait \(z\in\mathbb{R}\) produces offspring with traits distributed normally with mean \(z\) and variance \(\eta^2\). Assuming no selection or any process other than mutation, if \(\phi_t\) is the distribution of traits in one generation then

\[\phi_{t+1}(z)=\int_\mathbb{R}\phi_t(\zeta)\frac{\exp\left( -\frac{(z-\zeta)^2}{2\eta^2} \right)}{\sqrt{2\pi\eta^2}}d\zeta.\]

In the language of math, we say that mutation acts as a mollifier on the phenotypic density. The equivalent continuous time version of this is diffusion, that is,

\[\frac{\partial\phi_t}{\partial t}=\eta^2\frac{\partial^2\phi_t}{\partial z^2}.\]

The above discrete time dynamics of \(\sigma^2\) with random mating and mutation become \(\Delta\sigma^2=\eta^2-\frac{\sigma^2}{2}\). One way to show this is to take the same approach as above but assume the offpsring phenotypic density takes the form

\[\phi_o(z)=\sum_iw_i\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\eta^2}}\exp\left( -\frac{(m_i-z)^2}{2\eta^2} \right).\]

Another way would be to assume \(\phi_t\) is normal and integrate the equation \(\partial_t\phi_t=\eta^2\partial_{zz}\phi_t+\phi(\ln\phi_t-\overline{\ln\phi_t})\) over a single unit of time. I leave the details to the motivated reader.

Returning to the perspective of dispersal as geographic mutation, we can replace \(\eta\) with \(m\), where I’m now using \(m\) to represent the standard deviation in location between offspring and parent.

Population Dynamics

We need to return to considering the effects of mating on the population dynamics.

Drift

Note that we have already obtained results for stochastic demographic dynamics here. Can we use these results to obtain a model for drift? Or are the stochastic dynamics of random reproductive ability somehow inherently distinct from drift? Drift is commonly modelled as the random sampling of the adults that have survived viability selection to produce a gene pool. This of course only makes sense for a sexually reproducing population. Then does drift have no effect on clonally reproducing populations? Another odd point is that the variance due to demographic dynamics grows with population size. As each individual contributes some stochasticity to the total number of individuals in the next generation, it makes sense that the variance of this number grows with population size. But this is the opposite effect of drift, which decays with increasing population size. So then maybe the two really are distinct. In such a case we could incorporate an additional component to fitness, a Bernoulli random variable that decides whether the individual is included in the gene pool or not.

\[\mathcal{W}\delta,\ \mathcal{W}\sim\mathcal{P}(W_t), \ \delta\sim\mathrm{Bern}(p)\]

To satisfy the principle of maximum entropy we can set \(p=0.5\), making \(\mathbb{E}(N_e)=N_t/2\), where \(N_e\) is the number of individuals chosen for the gene pool. If \(N_e\) is an integer we can derive \(\xi_t\sim\mathcal{N}(0,G/N_e)\) where \(G\) is the additive genetic variance. Hand-waving may now occur to justify this result for any positive \(N_e\). Note that as \(N_e\rightarrow0\) bad things occur. But it doesn’t make sense to have \(N_e<1\) since our population would then be extinct.

Stochastics

I focus on continuous time stochastic dynamics because it’s difficult to tell what distribution \(\overline{\mathcal{W}}_t\) has. If we are consistent with everything derived up until this point we run into some trouble. Namely, since the individual fitnesses \(\mathcal{W}\) have been modelled as Poisson random variables, they can take the value zero with positive probability. Hence, \(\ln\mathcal{W}\) can be negatively infinite with positive probability. Thus \(\mathbb{E}(\ln\mathcal{W})\) is undefined. We might be able to formally deal with this situation using a generalization of the central limit theorem that would lead us to the so called stable distributions. Alternatively, we can sweep this dirt under the rug and assume that \(\ln\mathcal{W}_t(i)\) is distributed normally with mean \(\ln W_t(i)\) and variance \(V_t(i)\). Then \(\overline{\ln\mathcal{W}_t}\sim\mathcal{N}(\overline{\ln W_t},\overline{V_t})\) where

\[\overline{\ln W_t}=\sum_i\ln W_t(i)\phi_t(i), \ \overline{V_t}=\sum_iV_t\phi_t^2(i).\]

This leads to the SDE

\[d\phi_t(i)=(\ln W_t(i)-\overline{\ln W_t})\phi_t(i)dt+\sqrt{V_t(i)+\overline{V_t}}\phi_t(i)dB_t\]

which defines geometric Brownian motion.

Instead making the above assumptions, incorporating diffusive mutation into the mix can salvage the formality of the approach. Specifically, since diffusion has infinite propogation speed, if \(\phi_t(z)>0\) for some \(z\) at time \(t=t_0\) and

\[\frac{\partial\phi_t}{\partial t}=\phi_t\left( \ln W_t-\overline{\ln W_t} \right)+\eta^2\frac{\partial^2\phi_t}{\partial z^2}\]

then \(\phi_t>0\) for every \(z\) and every \(t>t_0\).
